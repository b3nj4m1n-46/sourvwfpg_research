Federal / Workforce Pell Grant

A new Pell category for short, high-quality, employment-aligned programs

program shape
  • 8–15 weeks, 150–599 clock hours
  • No correspondence, noncredit, or remedial coursework
  • Must be offered by an accredited institution
  • Must award academic credit toward an existing certificate or degree
three annual accountability tests
  • 70% of participants complete within 150% of normal time
  • 70% of completers employed in the 2nd quarter after exit
  • Tuition + fees ≤ median completer earnings − 150% of poverty line (value-added earnings; ≈ $23,475 in 2026)
provisions that favor universities
  • Bachelor's-degree holders are eligible
  • Employers may deliver up to 25% of instruction (49% via Registered Apprenticeship)
  • Governor + state Workforce Board approve first, then U.S. Secretary of Education
key timeline
  • Jul 4, 2025 — OBBBA §83002 signed
  • Mar 9, 2026 — NPRM published
  • May 19, 2026 — Final rule published
  • Jul 20, 2026 — Rule effective (early opt-in Jul 1)
  • AY 2026/27 – 2028/29 — Interim phase: only 70/70 applies
  • AY 2029/30 — Transition: first value-added earnings calcs
  • AY 2030/31+ — Full accountability
Sources: OBBBA §83002 • ED Final Rule Fact Sheet, May 18, 2026 • D² Strategies (Manhattan Inst.), April 2026.

Position / why 4-year universities, and the proof

Workforce Pell was written for institutions like SOU

the distinctive 4-year market
  • Bachelor's-degree holders are eligible — rare in Pell. Career-changers, alumni, second-degree candidates.
  • Countercyclical to the enrollment cliff: traditional 18-year-olds decline; adult re-skilling grows with AI & industry transformation.
  • 41.9M Americans aged 25–64 have some college but no credential (SCNC).
  • Four-year accreditation, stackable credit, and bachelor's-ladder pathways are moats that for-profit short-course providers cannot cross.
STATES RUNNING WORKFORCE PELL LIKE PROGRAMS
  • Virginia — $140.8M New Economy Workforce Credential Grant: 90% completion, 67% earn industry credentials, 60%+ had no prior postsecondary.
  • Tennessee — $1.5B coordinated short-credential-to-degree pathway model.
  • Texas — $605.7M performance-based community-college funding.
  • Iowa, Louisiana — robust noncredit + wage-record data systems already exist.

Oregon's state-level data & approval infrastructure is not yet built.
SOU can be the regional model.

Authority / Oregon defines its own in-demand list

HECC and WTDB shape what counts — the program list is dynamic

what the federal rule actually says
  • Each Governor must publish a methodology for identifying high-skill, high-wage, or in-demand occupations (§ 690.93(b)).
  • Only one of the three bars must clear. An occupation can qualify on high-skill alone.
  • Lists must be reviewed every two years — designed to evolve.
  • U.S. Dept. of Ed.: “Eligible programs will vary across states based on local labor market needs.”
Oregon's foundation is already in place
  • The 2026 Oregon Talent Assessment (ECOnorthwest → HECC) is explicitly the methodology starting point for Workforce Pell.
  • 15 in-demand sectors including Forestry & Wood Products (LQ 3.1), Clean Energy / Climate Tech, Social Assistance / Care Economy.
  • 209 priority occupations mapped against five criteria.
  • SOU's environmental, fire, and teaching strengths fit multiple sectors on that list.

The list is not fixed. Programs SOU brings forward can be added — if HECC and WTDB are at the table.

And the Governor has directed the state to do exactly that:
“Amplify regional economic development strategies and support local efforts to utilize unique regional resources, talent pools, and market opportunities.”
— Oregon's Prosperity Roadmap, Dec 2025 (next slide)

Sources: Workforce Pell NPRM § 690.93(b); U.S. Dept. of Ed., Dec 9, 2025 briefing; 2026 Oregon Talent Assessment.

Operating model / structurally a public-private partnership

Workforce Pell is, by design, a public-private partnership

The federal rule does not let an institution run a Workforce Pell program alone. Three partnerships are statutory:

  • Government. Governor + State Workforce Board certify alignment to high-skill, high-wage, in-demand occupations. Re-cert every two years.
  • Employers. May deliver up to 25% of instruction (49% in Registered Apprenticeship). May co-fund tuition. Hiring commitments support the 70% placement test.
  • Institution. Accreditation, curriculum, credit, outcomes tracking, advising, and academic ladder.

“Treat employers as co-investors in student success.” (D² Strategies, 2026)

Engine / data fusion as institutional asset

The data fusion layer:  Ashland Intelligence

The 2026 Oregon Talent Assessment calls explicitly for an “Oregon Talent Dashboard.” Ashland Intelligence is the regional prototype.

THE PICTURE TODAY: FRAGMENTED DATA PICTURE

The pieces exist. The firm that fuses them is already operating in the Rogue Valley.

  • Annotated Ledger — SOU budget dashboard, already built. Internal financial visibility.
  • RWP / WIOA reporting — regional outcomes published quarterly through HECC. Already exists.
  • HECC WIOA dashboard — public, but covers only WIOA-funded participants; no employer demand, no per-program ROI, no sub-board geography, 2-year lag.
  • Autom Insights — the connective tissue. Architecture-first infrastructure for ingesting and fusing disparate sources; already built SOREDI's regional economic data explorer.
WHAT WE PROPOSE TO BUILD: ASHLAND INTELLIGENCE

Oregon's state data infrastructure isn't ready. SOU builds the regional model; the state can scale it.

  • Ashland Intelligence — the regional dashboard. Joins employer demand, institutional capacity, and federal predicates on one schema; cites every source. Will provide API + MCP tools for a federated query layer plus an agent-accessible interface for advising, intake, and reporting.
  • White Rabbit / ROVA — AI agent infrastructure proven to harvest structured data from disparate sources. Recruits Rogue Valley employers and runs scheduled surveys to keep the demand side current.

Statewide Scalable Model  designed so EOU, OSU-Cascades, COCC, Lane CC, and other Oregon institutions can deploy the same layer.

Key assumption: SOU only launches programs that repackage existing faculty and facilities. No HVAC, no new clinical labs, no specialized hires — those stay with RCC or, for nursing, the SOU / OHSU Ashland partnership.

What this is & isn't

Is: recurring net contribution to SOU operations; conservative cohort sizes; existing faculty.
Isn't: for-profit-style profit; one-time program-development cost (~$5–15k each); marketing rampup; cannibalization of degree enrollment.
